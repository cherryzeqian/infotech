Core Ideas About Nature

These cards explain four big ideas that help our Living Garden work: trees, ecosystems, photosynthesis, and seasons. Each card has a short story to make the science easy to understand.

Trees

Trees are tall plants with strong trunks that support branches and leaves. Their roots drink water and take in nutrients from the soil. Leaves help trees breathe by taking in carbon dioxide and releasing oxygen, which people and animals need to live. Trees also make shade, protect soil from washing away in the rain, and give homes to birds and insects. When we plant or care for trees, we help the whole environment stay healthy.

Ecosystems

An ecosystem is a community of living things—plants, animals, and tiny organisms—plus sunlight, water, air, and soil. These parts interact and keep each other in balance. If one part changes, the others may change too. Healthy ecosystems provide clean air and water, food, and safe homes for wildlife. People can support ecosystems by keeping parks and gardens clean and by choosing plants that belong in local habitats.

Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is how plants make their own food. Leaves use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide from the air to create sugars for energy. Oxygen is released as a bonus. This process is one reason plants are so important for life on Earth—they make much of the oxygen we breathe. When days are cloudy or short, plants may grow more slowly. That is why seasons and weather matter to gardens, farms, and forests.

Seasons

Seasons are parts of the year with different temperatures and weather patterns. In many places, spring brings new growth, summer brings warmth and longer days, fall brings colorful leaves, and winter brings cold and rest for many plants. Not every region has the same seasons, and places near the equator can feel warm most of the year. Learning about local seasons helps us plan when to plant, water, and harvest.

How to Use These Cards

Start with any bar that looks interesting and tap it to open. Read the short story and imagine a real place you know—a garden, a schoolyard, or a park—where you can see this idea in action.

  • Trees: Think about one tree you see often. What lives on or under it?
  • Ecosystems: Notice how plants, animals, and weather all connect.
  • Photosynthesis: Look for sunny and shady spots. How do plants look different?
  • Seasons: Try to name the current season and what is changing right now.

1) What Is a Living Garden?

A living garden is a small world full of life. Plants, animals, insects, and tiny organisms all depend on each other. Plants give food and shelter. Animals spread seeds. Insects help plants make new flowers and fruit. Even very small creatures, like worms and microbes, play important roles.

“A living garden is a small ______ where many living things depend on each other.”

2) What Do Plants Need?

Plants need sunlight, water, air, and soil to live. Sunlight gives energy. Water carries nutrients through the plant. Air, especially carbon dioxide, helps plants make food. Soil holds the plant in place and gives minerals. Plants use these parts in a process called photosynthesis to make their own food and release oxygen.

Fill-in-the-blank:
“Plants use sunlight, water, and air in a process called ______ to make their own food.”

3) Pollinators: Garden Helpers

Pollinators are animals that move pollen from one flower to another. This helps plants make seeds and fruits. Bees are famous pollinators, but butterflies, moths, birds, and some small mammals help too. When a pollinator visits a flower to drink nectar, some pollen sticks to its body and then moves to the next flower.

Fill-in-the-blank:
“Pollinators help plants make seeds by moving ______ between flowers.”

4) Soil: Not Just Dirt

Soil looks like plain dirt, but it is full of life. Good soil has tiny rocks, old plant pieces, water, air, worms, insects, fungi, and bacteria. These small organisms break down dead leaves and turn them into nutrients that plants can use. Soil also holds roots and stores water. Healthy soil helps plants grow strong.

Fill-in-the-blank:
“Soil is important because it is full of tiny living things, like worms and ______.”

5) Seasons in the Garden

In spring, plants wake up and grow new leaves. In summer, they grow fast in the warm light. In fall, leaves change color and many plants drop seeds. In winter, many plants rest and the garden is quiet. Each season brings different colors and jobs for plants, animals, and people.

Fill-in-the-blank:
“In spring, many plants begin to grow new ______ after winter.”

6) Why Gardens Are Important

Gardens are important for people, animals, and the planet. They give us food like fruits and vegetables. They offer homes for insects, birds, and other wildlife. Gardens help clean the air by taking in carbon dioxide and giving out oxygen. They also make neighborhoods cooler and more peaceful. Even small gardens can support pollinators and reduce waste when we compost.
